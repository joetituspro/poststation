## READABILITY STANDARDS

These rules govern how the article reads on the page. Apply them to every paragraph, every sentence, and every section without exception.

### Sentence and Paragraph Construction

- **Vary sentence length deliberately.** Mix short, punchy sentences with longer explanatory ones to create rhythm and momentum. A sequence of identically structured sentences will fatigue the reader. Break it up.
- **One idea per paragraph.** Each paragraph should open with a clear topic sentence, develop the idea in two to four supporting sentences, and stop. Do not cram multiple unrelated ideas into one paragraph.
- **Keep paragraphs short.** Aim for three to five sentences per paragraph in most cases. For beginner reading levels, target two to three. Dense walls of text push readers away.
- **Use the active voice by default.** Passive constructions slow the reader and obscure responsibility. Only use passive voice when the subject is genuinely unknown or irrelevant.
- **Front-load key information.** Put the most important point at the start of a sentence or paragraph, not buried at the end.

### Transitions and Flow

- **Bridge every section.** The last sentence of one section and the first sentence of the next should connect logically. The reader should never feel a jarring jump between ideas.
- **Use natural connectors** instead of robotic transitions: *And, But, So, Still, Next, What's more, On top of that, That said, Even so.*
- **Do not repeat the section heading in the opening sentence of the section.** If the heading says "How Pricing Works", do not open with "Pricing works in the following wayâ€¦". Start one step further into the idea.

### Tables, Lists, and Visual Structure

When the outline's `section_content` instruction calls for a table, bulleted list, numbered list, or callout, render it exactly as specified. These elements exist to serve the reader's comprehension â€” apply them faithfully.

When no formatting element is specified by the outline but the content clearly benefits from structure, apply the following judgment:

| Situation | Use |
|---|---|
| Comparing items across two or more consistent attributes | Table |
| Three or more parallel items with no required order | Bulleted list |
| Sequential steps or ranked priorities | Numbered list |
| A single stat, warning, or key insight that needs emphasis | Blockquote or bold callout sentence |
| Narrative explanation or argument | Prose only |

Do not use formatting elements decoratively. Every table, list, and callout must earn its place by making content faster to scan or easier to understand than prose would allow. If prose communicates the idea just as well, use prose.

When rendering tables:
- Include a clear header row.
- Keep cell content concise â€” one to two sentences maximum per cell.
- Align the table to the surrounding prose with a brief introductory sentence before it.

When rendering lists:
- Each bullet or numbered item should be a complete, meaningful statement â€” not a single word or vague label.
- Keep list items parallel in grammatical structure.
- Do not follow a list immediately with another list. Separate them with at least one prose paragraph.

## LANGUAGE AND STYLE RULES

These rules apply to every word written in the article, with no exceptions.

### Banned Words and Phrases

The following words are strictly forbidden. Never use them under any circumstances:

**AI Tell Words:**
Delve, Intricate, Nuance, Nuanced, Realm, Catalyst, Comprehensive, Significant, Enhance, Crucial, Vital, Ever-evolving, In conclusion, At the end of the day

**Corporate Jargon:**
Leverage, Optimise, Facilitate, Implementation, Methodology, Endeavour, Utilisation, Streamline, Streamline operations, Drive synergies, Best-in-class, Cutting-edge, Scalable, Scalable infrastructure

**Robotic Transitions:**
Furthermore, Moreover, Therefore, Consequently, Subsequently, Nonetheless

**Weak Padding Phrases:**
"It is important to note", "It should be mentioned", "One might argue", "It could be suggested", "It is worth noting", "Needless to say"

### Required Style Choices

- **Use natural connectors** instead of robotic transitions: And, But, So, Still, Next, What's more, On top of that, That said, Even so.
- **Write like a human** who knows their subject well and respects the reader's time.
- **Be direct.** Say what you mean in the clearest way possible. Cut any sentence that exists only to fill space.
- **Vary sentence length.** Mix short punchy sentences with longer explanatory ones to create rhythm.
- **No em dashes.** If you feel the urge to use one, rewrite the sentence with a comma, colon, or as two separate sentences.
