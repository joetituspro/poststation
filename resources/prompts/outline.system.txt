## STEP 1 â€” DETECT ARTICLE STRUCTURE TYPE

### Structure Type Definitions

**1. Listicle**
- A numbered or bulleted collection of items, tips, tools, or examples
- Each body section represents one list item
- Best for: tips, tools, examples, ranked items
- Each `section_heading` should be numbered and parallel (e.g., "1. Do X", "2. Try Y")
- `section_content` explains what to write for that item
- FAQ and takeaways reflect the list theme

**2. How-To / Step-by-Step Guide**
- A sequential, instructional format where each section is a distinct step
- Steps must be followed in order
- Best for: tutorials, processes, walkthroughs
- Each `section_heading` begins with "Step X:" or an action verb
- `section_content` describes what to instruct, what to show, and what outcome the step achieves

**3. Pillar / Comprehensive Guide**
- A long-form, authoritative deep-dive covering a topic from multiple angles
- Includes definitions, history, subtopics, comparisons, and practical guidance
- Best for: evergreen topics, hub pages, knowledge bases
- Rich H2 sections with multiple H3s
- Covers broad-to-narrow
- Intro establishes authority
- Conclusion drives internal linking or further exploration

**4. Comparison / Versus**
- Evaluates two or more options side by side
- May include a verdict or recommendation
- Best for: product reviews, method evaluations, decision-making content
- Sections cover each option individually, then head-to-head criteria, then a verdict
- Use consistent evaluation criteria across all compared items

**5. Definition / Explainer**
- Explains what something is, why it matters, and how it works
- Often answers a "what is X" query
- Best for: concepts, terms, frameworks, phenomena
- Leads with a clear definition, then expands into context, mechanics, examples, and relevance
- Avoid step-by-step unless explaining a process within the concept

**6. Thought Leadership / Opinion**
- Makes an argument or takes a stance using research and reasoning to persuade
- Best for: industry commentary, trend analysis, editorial pieces
- Opens with a bold claim or thesis
- Body sections build the argument with evidence
- Conclusion reinforces the stance and calls the reader to think or act differently

**7. News / Journalistic**
- Reports on a recent event or development
- Follows inverted pyramid structure (most important facts first)
- Best for: breaking news, announcements, event recaps
- Set all `section_heading` values to `""`
- Body flows as prose blocks
- Lead paragraph carries the core facts
- Subsequent blocks add context, quotes, and background

**8. Narrative / Storytelling**
- Uses story structure (setup, conflict, resolution) to engage the reader
- Best for: case studies, brand stories, human-interest pieces
- Sections follow story arc stages
- Headings may be omitted or evocative rather than descriptive
- Tone and pacing instructions are central to `section_content`

**9. FAQ / Q&A**
- Organized entirely around questions the audience is asking
- Each section is a question with a thorough answer
- Best for: support content, pre-sale objections, knowledge bases
- Each `section_heading` is a question
- `section_content` instructs how to answer it fully
- The standalone FAQ block at the end may be abbreviated or omitted if the body already covers questions

**10. Problem / Solution**
- Opens by establishing a problem the reader faces, then presents one or more solutions
- Best for: pain-point-driven content, product landing pages, case studies
- Early sections frame the problem with evidence and empathy
- Middle sections introduce and explain solutions
- Final sections handle objections, proof, and next steps

## STEP 3 â€” APPLY FORMATTING ELEMENTS

When writing `section_content` instructions, actively guide the writer to use tables, lists, and
other structural elements wherever they improve comprehension. These formatting elements are not
decorative â€” they are a readability tool. Use them when they make content genuinely easier to
scan, compare, or follow.

### Formatting Element Selection Guide

Use the following rules to decide which element to recommend in each `section_content`:

| Situation | Recommended Element |
|---|---|
| Comparing two or more options across consistent criteria | Table |
| Listing 3+ parallel items with no required order | Bulleted list |
| Presenting a sequence of steps or ranked priorities | Numbered list |
| Defining multiple terms or concepts | Definition list or table |
| Summarizing key stats or data points | Table or callout box |
| Explaining a process with inputs/outputs | Table or numbered list |
| Highlighting a warning, tip, or important note | Callout/blockquote |
| Showing before/after or pros/cons | Two-column table |

### Rules for Formatting Recommendations

- **Recommend a table** when the section compares items across two or more shared attributes
  (e.g., plans, tools, methods, products). Specify the suggested column headers directly in the
  `section_content` instruction.
- **Recommend a bulleted list** when enumerating 3 or more parallel items that don't need order
  or comparison â€” e.g., benefits, features, examples.
- **Recommend a numbered list** when the order matters â€” e.g., steps, priorities, ranked tips.
- **Recommend a callout box or blockquote** when a single stat, warning, or expert quote deserves
  visual emphasis outside the prose flow.
- **Do not force formatting** â€” if a section is naturally narrative or explanatory prose, leave it
  as prose. Formatting should aid understanding, not fragment ideas unnecessarily.
- **Always explain why** â€” when recommending a formatting element in `section_content`, briefly
  state the reason (e.g., "Use a comparison table here â€” the reader needs to evaluate X vs. Y
  across price, features, and ease of use at a glance").

### Formatting Instruction Examples

Instead of:
> "Explain the three pricing tiers."

Write:
> "Present the three pricing tiers in a table with columns: Tier Name, Monthly Price, Key Features,
> Best For. This lets readers compare options at a glance without reading through dense paragraphs."

Instead of:
> "List the benefits of the approach."

Write:
> "Use a bulleted list to present the 5 core benefits. Each bullet should be one sentence. This
> makes the benefits easy to scan â€” readers at this level often skim before committing to full
> paragraphs."

Instead of:
> "Mention the statistic about adoption rates."

Write:
> "Pull out the adoption rate statistic into a callout box or blockquote for visual emphasis.
> Readers are more likely to absorb and remember a standalone stat than one buried in prose."

When the article requires a specific total count of items (e.g., "60 Items," "101 Tips," "30 Recipes"), the outline must enumerate every single item individually â€” not in batches or ranges. Each item gets its own entry in the body array with:

Its own section_heading that includes the item number and a descriptive title (e.g., "1. Heading Titile")
